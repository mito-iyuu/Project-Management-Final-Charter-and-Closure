STAR Stories

Problem-solving and decision-making situations

  • Situation: During a data analytics group project, our group was having difficulties with the size of our data that was going to be used for the analysis. The main issue stemmed from the fact that I had already vastly cleaned and reduced the data. There was worry that there wouldn't be enough substance to create analysis and visualizations from. With the due date approaching, we needed to solve this issue first and foremost.
  • Task: My role was to clean the data and start working on visualizations using Elastic Search and Kibana (data analysis programs).
  • Action: The first time I reduced the data was mostly by reducing the rows and shortening the time frame. This wasn‚Äôt enough, so after looking at the rows of data and brainstorming solutions, we decided to keep the range within 2025. I decided to reduce the data all the way to 2025, reducing columns, but to keep our analysis capabilities versatile, I brought back rows, which brought us to a perfect compromise.
  • Result: The issue was successfully resolved through quick brainstorming and finding ways to come to a compromise. We were still able to conduct an interesting analysis, despite it not being what we initially wanted. I learned that sacrifices will need to be made to meet requirements, regardless of what you want. Making the best decision for the overall project's success, rather than prioritizing your own wants, is the most important priority.

Team collaboration and communication strategies

  • Situation: During a group project, all tasks were behind schedule with the due date approaching. The main issue was not having even started the website prototype that needed to be presented. The issue was critical, and there was no communication happening between the members.
  • Task: I was responsible for resolving the communication issue and making sure everyone is on the same page so we can complete our project.
  • Action: I reached out to everyone separately as well as in the group chat, first reminding them of the deadline and the tasks we have yet to complete. I asked if this issue was happening because they had other projects, and they confirmed, sharing their schedules. With this, we started a discussion about what each of us could do to get this project done. I compromised and took over creating the prototype, while my members supported me when and where they could.
  • Result: The issue was resolved through bettering our communication and clarifying our schedules so we could more clearly plan when we could each complete our tasks. I learned that things can quickly go off schedule and that we should be more prepared for such cases to happen. I also learned that when such things happen, sacrifices will need to be made.

Demonstrate leadership behaviors

  • Situation: During a group data analytics project, I took notice of a member who was struggling to keep up with the pace of the project and tasks that needed to be done.
  • Task: I took the responsibility to allocate tasks.
  • Action: I took action by first checking in on the member to see if there was an issue. I followed this by giving a slower paced task that was due far later that the deliverables needed for the project. We redistributed tasks making sure it was something each of the members were able to complete.
  • Result: We were able to smoothly complete the project while easing the burden on the member that was struggling. I learned that it is important to accomodate certain situations and be ready to have changes in plans.

Create a collaborative project team environment

  • Situation: During a group project requiring us to first choose a data set to analyze, we couldn't decide on a single dataset. There were many suggestion sbut we were unable to stick to one.
  • Task: I took the responsibility to bring the discussion to the same page.
  • Action: I started the discussion, emphasizing that we need to decide soon. The first thing I did was bring the discussion down to a smaller scale of topic. What topic were we interested in the most. We then discussed the requirements needed for the data, and from there it was easier to decide a dataset.
  • Result: We were quickly able to decide our data set. The issue was starting off the discussion too broad, as everyone was suggesting data sets without confirming topic interest. I learned that taking discussions step by step will help ensure we all are on the same page and can decide as a team what step to take.

Navigate Complexity

  • Situation: During a data analytics project, our team was analyzing customer behavior trends for a simulated company. Midway through the project, our instructor introduced the second part of the porject where we were required to integrate a second dataset. This new dataset used different naming conventions, overlapping variables, etc. The sudden addition created confusion, and the team wasn‚Äôt sure how to merge both datasets.
  • Task: I took the responsibility to play around with the data set and research ways to combine the data.
  • Action: I analyzed the rows in both data sets, identifying any overlap that we could use to combine the data. Using the ID row, we combined the data using only which IDs were present in both. We also cleaned the data, ensuring that the data were in the same format so their wouldn't be any issues during analysis.
  • Result: We successfully integrated both datasets and adapted our analysis to the new requirements without missing any deadlines. Despite my group having no experience, we were able to solve this by stepping back, breaking steps into smaller pieces, and slowly solving the problem piece by piece.
